My wife gave birth to our daughter on a cold Autumn eve when the Japanese invaded Manchuria. She was naked, red, and crying for breath. A child so small she could have fit into the crook of my arm

I can feel her in my arms, these feelings of safety and security taking me over. Vulnerability seemed a far distant thought that was further away than beaches of Iwo Jima. Yet, something was coming to find me, to pull me away from this false notion of peace.

Pearl Harbor.

17. 18. 19. These men are still children. How the fuck can a child be a soldier when he’s never laid beside a girl in the same bed. How the fuck can a child be a soldier if he’s cheeks are still chubby since he was 5? I see these children leaping from the amphibious vehicles like frogs springing from one log to the next as the hawk o’er head gazes down waiting to pluck. 17. 18. 19. dead men.

 I made a vow to God. If I made it out of this war alive, I would carry my youngest daughter on my back to stairs of the cathedral to be baptized.

My wife keeps asking me, “When will you return?” When will I return?

“Soon,” I said, “once the infantry leaves the shores of Iwo Jima. I’ll be home before the end of the summer.” We’re preparing for the invasion of Japan.

“She walked for the first time today. A few bumbling steps, but she marches forward.”

How many days has it been since we landed on this fucking hellhole? I don’t know. The days blur together.

These men are of the old breed, these men are our commanding officer. The very men put in charge of our lives. I can now see white sands of Pacific before me.
