A Cold Autumn Eve

My wife gave birth to our daughter on a cold Autumn eve when the Japanese invaded Manchuria. She was naked, red, and crying for breath. A child so small she could have fit into the crook of my arm.

I can feel her in my arms, these feelings of safety and security taking me over. Vulnerability seemed a far distant thought that was further away than the beaches of Iwo Jima. Yet, something was coming to find me, to pull me away from this false notion of peace. Pearl Harbor.

Background

The Battle of Guadalcanal

The Battle of Guadalcanal was fought between August 7th, 1942 to 9th February 1943. On the seventh of August, Alied forces landed on Guadalcanval with the objective of hindering the Japanese progression in the Pacific and to threathen enemy supploy and communication routes throughout the Pacific. The Japanese had occupied the island since May 1942 and were severely outnumbered by the Allies

Shocked by the Allied offensive, the Japanese tried to retake their airfields, engaging in three major land battlles and several large naval battles. Each side bombared the other leaving a total of 7,100 Allied forces dead and 31,000 Japanese soldiers dead.

Guadalcanal was the decisive turning point in the war. The Japanese surrendered the island in the early year.

The Rains Came

November 10 1943

This afternoon, while we were south of Bougainville ... we came across a raft with four live Japs in it ... As the destroyer Spence came close to the raft, the Japs opened up with a machine gun at the destroyer. The Jap officer then put the gun in each man’s mouth and fired, blowing out the back of each man’s skull. One of the Japs did not want to die for the Emperor and put up a struggle. The others held him down. The officer was the last to die. He also blew his brains out. The Spence went in to investigate. All the bodies had disappeared into the water. There was nothing left but blood and an empty raft. Swarms of sharks were everywhere. The sharks ate well today ... We went to battle stations ... and at 10PM we were attacked by enemy planes ... Later darkness descended and the rains came.

September 3, 1942

Guadalcanal Island

Dear Mother, Dad, Katharine, and John: Yesterday we got our first mail, the best birthday present possible for me. ... After mail call everybody would be nice and quiet when suddenly somebody would curse in a loud voice and shout, “Alice got married.” It really was funny. Cherokee got word that he is out in the cold and really surprised us all. One fellow in our squad got a box of cookies that had been reduced to dust and the dust was soon reduced leaving an empty box…. Daddy! You absent-minded prof. When you write to mother, you better mail it to her and not accidentally put it in my letter. I destroyed it and didn’t show it to the boys though, just to show what a sport I am… Tell everybody hello for me…

Love

Sid

Smell of Pine

Guadalcanal Island

While we were giving them hell, the Japs somehow managed to land their amphibious vechicles on the beach and outpoured a battalion of men. The gunnery sergeant yelled at us to get into formation to defend the beach from their invasions. I ran post the pine trees lining the beach and the sweet smell of pine reminded me of the years passing me by.

We ran down from the beach back to the guns. Four minutes later hell brooke loose as the other Japs landed on the beach. We offered them our finest bullets, hoping some would nail them in the head or heart. I want to forget the smell of sweet pine intermingled with blood. I want to forget the sound of men screaming like panic horses being fired at. I want to forget this shithole of an island.

When the light headed back to the moutains we began to prepare for the long run. This is going to be a long and bloody night.



Background

European Campaign spanned across many countries. Allied forces tredged through muck and mire, climbed over barbwire, all while getting blozen to bits by Nazi artillery.

The Battle of the Bulge was fought between December 16, 1944 to January 25, 1945. It was a major German offensive that took place in Belgium, France, and Luxembourg. The Germans decided to take on a weakly defended section of the Allied line, a suprise attack that took them offguard.

Although the Germans had the upper edge, they were defeated after the Allies brought in reinforcements. The battle involved 610,000 American men, 19,000 which were killed and 89,000 casualities. It was the largest and bloodiest battle fought by the U.S. during the war.

Sweating things out

Belgium, November 19, 1944

I hardly know how to begin after such a long time and I really have been sweating it out. But speaking of sweating things out, in the past two weeks there was a few mornings that really called for a good deal of sweating out. It used to be fairly peaceful to lay in our foxholes but these particular mornings there was plenty of big stuff falling nearby. I never was too scared of the stuff until then. We happened to be about eight miles inside of Reich and the artillery was coming from all directions. Everytime a shell started to whistle in, I was beginning another prayer. As one of the 'doughfeet' put it, "I may not get the Purple Heart for being wounded but if they give them out for being scared as hell I certainly rate one," and that's no kiddin'...

Respectfully, Carl Schluter


Virginian Snow

We've had little rest in the past two days. Every man's head slowly nods in a foxhole as sleep tries taking cover. Heavy eyelids feel like steel shutters shutting out the bright white snow.

I think it's Tuesday. Perhaps, it's Monday. The days blur into each other. It's snowed past four days.



After Post

I reckon this will be the last of you’ll ever hear of me. Haven't got much time seeing Death is looming o’er head of me in this pending hour. Us soldiers evade Death and all his friends like birds flying south to avoid a cold New York winter. If there is one last thing you should know about me before I go is that I was never scared of death but being forgotten.

You see, all a man has when he is stripped naked and buried is a name. That name is his identity and legacy. I found myself that day I was wounded at Guadacanal. I learned that I was the type of man who refused to die. Although my life was comfortable in Texas, I would get this constant itch, a yearning to find myself again.

Soldiering ain’t what it’s made out to be, fancy uniforms and gold emblems. It’s the experience of lying in the mud, wounded, while your friends die around you.

I can still taste that Pacific soil and the cold warmth of rain and dirt on my back. Bury me there with the rest of my friends.
